Slack / Email Communication Norms Published v1

Download original Word document

Prepared By: Angela Tino, Executive Operations Lead

Last Updated: July 22, 2026

Applies To: All Canopi team members

1. Purpose

As a remote-first team, Slack and email are how most of Canopi's day-to-day work actually happens. This guide sets expectations for using them well — clear, considerate, and easy for a distributed team to keep up with.

2. Choosing the Right Channel

  • Slack: day-to-day team communication, quick questions, anything that benefits from a fast back-and-forth.

  • Email: external communication (candidates, vendors, partners), anything that needs a more formal or lasting record, and longer-form updates that don't need real-time discussion.

  • A meeting or call: anything that's gone back and forth more than a couple of times without landing, or that's sensitive/high-stakes enough to benefit from tone and real-time back-and-forth (see the Code of Conduct's Remote Work Conduct section).

3. Slack Norms

  • Use channels over DMs when the topic could be useful to others — default to visibility rather than private threads, unless the content is genuinely personal or sensitive.

  • Use threads to reply to a specific message, rather than starting a new top-level message — it keeps channels easy to follow.

  • Set your Slack status when you're away or heads-down, especially during your agreed working hours (see the Remote Work / Work-From-Home Policy).

  • A message doesn't need an immediate reply outside someone's working hours — respect the async nature of a distributed team.

4. Email Norms

  • Use a clear, specific subject line — “Quick question” tells the reader nothing.

  • Keep the To: line to people who need to act, and Cc anyone who just needs visibility.

  • For candidate or external communication, keep tone professional and on-brand — see Resources > Brand Assets and the email signature template.

5. Tone & Clarity

  • Assume good intent — tone is easy to misread in text, especially across a distributed, async team.

  • Write messages that are clear and complete enough to act on without a round of clarifying questions — include the context, not just the ask.

  • If something starts to feel tense or is going back and forth without resolving, move it to a call rather than continuing in text (see the Code of Conduct).

6. Availability & Response Time

This mirrors the Remote Work / Work-From-Home Policy: there's no company-wide fixed response-time requirement, but team members are expected to be responsive during their own agreed working hours, and to communicate proactively if they'll be slower than usual.

7. Urgent Matters

Slack and email are both fundamentally async — if something is truly time-sensitive (e.g. a production incident, an urgent access need), say so explicitly, and consider a call or text message rather than relying on someone seeing a Slack message right away.

8. Suggested Channel Structure

A starting set of channels, to keep things organized without over-fragmenting into too many narrow ones:

Channel Purpose
#general Company-wide announcements and anything relevant to everyone
#access-requests Requesting access to tools/systems (per the IT/System Access Request SOP)
#engineering Engineering team discussion
#product Product team discussion
#random Non-work chat — optional, keeps #general focused on actual work

Note: This is a suggested starting structure, not a fixed list — adjust it to match whatever channels already exist in Canopi's workspace today.
